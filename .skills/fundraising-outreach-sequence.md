# Ariyo Fundraising Outreach Sequence

## Core Contract

Ariyo Fundraising Outreach Sequence is not a generic investor-email template, founder CRM checklist, or fundraising-growth hack.

It is the governed outbound, warm-intro, follow-up, meeting, evidence-sharing, objection-handling, and data-room-timing system that converts Ariyo's product narrative, pilot proof, demo assets, enterprise readiness, and investor data room into a disciplined fundraising motion.

Ariyo must not contact investors with scattered claims.
Ariyo must contact investors with a sequenced evidence path.

---

## 1. Purpose

This skill defines how Ariyo should run investor outreach after the core product story, MVP demo, pilot evidence, proof pack, and investor data room are ready enough to support serious conversations.

It tells founders, operators, advisors, Codex, AI-assisted builders, and fundraising support systems:

- which investor segments to target first;
- how to prioritize investor fit;
- when to send teaser, deck, demo, proof, and data room access;
- how to sequence warm intros and cold outreach;
- how to run follow-ups without sounding desperate;
- how to structure meeting flow;
- how to answer objections with evidence;
- how to track investor pipeline stages;
- what must never be claimed without proof.

## 2. Non-Negotiable Fundraising Rules

Ariyo fundraising outreach must follow these rules:

1. Do not start outreach with a long deck attachment.
2. Do not ask for money before establishing investor relevance.
3. Do not overclaim traction.
4. Do not call demo usage production revenue unless it is production revenue.
5. Do not describe Ariyo as a chatbot, wrapper, prompt marketplace, or generic automation platform.
6. Do not share full data room access before qualification.
7. Do not send sensitive pilot evidence without permission and redaction.
8. Do not use the same message for seed investors, enterprise SaaS investors, AI infra investors, marketplace investors, and strategic partners.
9. Do not chase uninterested investors beyond the defined follow-up limit.
10. Do not let investor feedback override customer evidence unless it maps to strategy.

## 3. Investor Targeting Tiers

### Tier 1 — Highest Fit Investors

Target first.

Investor profile:

- B2B SaaS experience;
- enterprise software experience;
- AI application or AI agent thesis;
- workflow automation familiarity;
- marketplace or ecosystem experience;
- willingness to invest before full production maturity;
- understanding of customer proof, not only revenue proof.

Best narrative:

> Ariyo is building the operating system for accountable AI employees, combining agent creation, workflow execution, marketplace distribution, governance, trust, and enterprise readiness.

### Tier 2 — Strategic AI / Enterprise Operators

Target after Tier 1 warm-up.

Investor profile:

- former enterprise SaaS operators;
- AI product leaders;
- automation platform operators;
- compliance/security-aware buyers;
- customer-success-led GTM operators.

Best narrative:

> Ariyo turns AI employees from experiments into governed, measurable, deployable operating units for businesses.

### Tier 3 — Marketplace / Platform Investors

Target when Marketplace story has enough proof.

Investor profile:

- marketplace investors;
- platform ecosystem investors;
- developer/creator economy investors;
- app store or partner ecosystem experience.

Best narrative:

> Ariyo is not only a product. It is an enterprise marketplace for trusted AI Employee operating assets.

### Tier 4 — Generalist Investors

Target only with a simplified story.

Investor profile:

- general seed/pre-seed investors;
- broad AI interest;
- limited enterprise detail appetite.

Best narrative:

> Companies want AI employees, but they need governance, workflows, trust, and measurable outcomes. Ariyo packages that into one operating platform.

## 4. Investor Fit Scoring

Each investor should be scored before outreach.

| Signal | Weight | Description |
|---|---:|---|
| Enterprise SaaS fit | 5 | Has invested in B2B workflow, SaaS, enterprise ops, security, data, or productivity |
| AI thesis fit | 5 | Has a clear thesis on AI agents, AI apps, automation, copilots, or workflow AI |
| Stage fit | 4 | Invests at Ariyo's current stage |
| Geography fit | 3 | Can invest in Ariyo's jurisdiction/market |
| Marketplace fit | 3 | Understands ecosystems, templates, app stores, partners, or marketplaces |
| Founder access | 3 | Warm intro, advisor path, network connection, or prior contact |
| Value-add | 3 | Can help with customers, enterprise sales, AI strategy, compliance, or follow-on funding |
| Speed | 2 | Known for responsive process |

Investor score:

- 22–28: Priority A
- 16–21: Priority B
- 10–15: Priority C
- below 10: Do not prioritize now

Codex must not generate investor outreach lists without scoring rationale.

## 8. Cold Email Sequence

### Email 1 — Relevance-first Intro

Subject options:

- Ariyo — AI Employee Operating System
- Governed AI employees for enterprise teams
- AI agents need an operating layer
- Building Ariyo: accountable AI employees

Body:

> Hi [Investor Name],
>
> I’m building Ariyo, an AI Employee Operating System for companies that want to move from AI experiments to accountable AI employees.
>
> Ariyo combines AI Employee creation, workflow execution, governance, monitoring, marketplace templates, and enterprise controls into one operating platform.
>
> I’m reaching out because you’ve invested in or written about [specific relevant thesis/company/category].
>
> We have a structured MVP/demo flow, pilot onboarding plan, and investor proof/data-room system prepared. I’d value a short conversation if this fits your AI/enterprise software thesis.
>
> Open to a 20-minute intro next week?
>
> Best,
> [Founder Name]

### Email 2 — Evidence Follow-up

Send 3–5 business days later.

Subject:

- Re: Ariyo
- Quick follow-up on Ariyo

Body:

> Hi [Investor Name],
>
> Quick follow-up.
>
> The core idea behind Ariyo is that companies will not only need AI agents — they will need a governed operating system for AI employees: permissions, workflows, outcomes, audits, marketplace assets, and enterprise controls.
>
> The demo covers:
>
> 1. creating an AI Employee;
> 2. assigning it to governed workflows;
> 3. monitoring outcomes, cost, and risk;
> 4. installing trusted marketplace templates;
> 5. showing enterprise admin and trust controls.
>
> Happy to share the short deck or walk through the demo if relevant.

### Email 3 — Concise Close-the-loop

Send 5–7 business days after Email 2.

Subject:

- Should I close the loop?

Body:

> Hi [Investor Name],
>
> I’ll close the loop after this note.
>
> Ariyo may be relevant if you’re looking at the shift from AI tools to governed AI operating systems for businesses.
>
> If that is an active thesis for you, I’d be glad to share the short deck and demo.
>
> If not, no worries.
>
> Best,
> [Founder Name]

Stop after Email 3 unless the investor engages.

## 11. Meeting Flow

### First Investor Meeting — 20 to 30 Minutes

Recommended flow:

1. Context — 2 minutes
2. Problem — 3 minutes
3. Ariyo product — 5 minutes
4. Demo path — 7 minutes
5. Market / why now — 4 minutes
6. Traction / proof status — 4 minutes
7. Ask / next step — 3 minutes

Do not overrun the meeting with product detail.
The goal is investor relevance and next-step qualification.

### Second Meeting — Product + Proof

Recommended flow:

1. Recap investor thesis alignment
2. Show MVP demo flow
3. Show pilot proof / planned proof system
4. Explain GTM wedge
5. Discuss market expansion
6. Discuss diligence questions
7. Share controlled data room access

### Third Meeting — Diligence / Partner Meeting

Recommended flow:

1. Evidence map
2. Architecture and trust posture
3. Pilot/customer proof
4. Team and execution roadmap
5. Financial model / fundraising ask
6. Risks and mitigations
7. Decision timeline

## 12. Data Room Timing

Ariyo must not share the full data room immediately.

### Stage-Based Sharing

| Stage | Share |
|---|---|
| Initial outreach | one-liner, short paragraph |
| Interest confirmed | teaser deck |
| First meeting booked | full deck or demo teaser |
| After first meeting | selected demo assets and proof summary |
| Serious diligence | controlled data room access |
| Partner interest | full data room + financials + roadmap + evidence |

### Redaction Rules

Before sharing:

- redact customer names unless approved;
- remove sensitive pricing terms;
- remove raw logs unless anonymized;
- remove internal credentials or operational details;
- summarize security posture without exposing vulnerabilities;
- separate public proof from confidential proof.

## 13. Objection Handling

### Objection: “Is this just another AI agent builder?”

Response:

> No. Ariyo is positioned as an operating system for accountable AI employees. The builder is only one surface. The platform also includes workflow execution, governance, permissions, monitoring, marketplace templates, trust controls, customer success, and release readiness.

Evidence to show:

- AI Employee Builder mockup/spec
- Workflow Builder mockup/spec
- Enterprise Admin mockup/spec
- Trust Center mockup/spec
- Product Surface Map

### Objection: “Where is the moat?”

Response:

> The moat is not a single model call. It is the operating layer: governed workflows, marketplace assets, enterprise trust, deployment evidence, policy controls, template certification, data/context systems, and customer outcome loops.

Evidence to show:

- AI Employee Marketplace skill
- Marketplace template lifecycle skills
- Enterprise governance skills
- Pilot feedback to roadmap system
- Post-pilot investor proof pack

### Objection: “Why now?”

Response:

> Companies are moving from AI experimentation to AI deployment, but deployment requires accountability, governance, monitoring, and repeatable operating assets. Ariyo is built for that transition.

Evidence to show:

- platform narrative
- MVP demo flow
- pilot onboarding plan
- production readiness gap audit

### Objection: “Is this enterprise-ready?”

Response:

> The MVP is not positioned as full production enterprise readiness. Ariyo separates demo readiness, pilot readiness, enterprise pilot readiness, and production readiness through release gates and gap audits.

Evidence to show:

- production-readiness-gap-audit.md
- production-release-gate-checklist.md
- enterprise-readiness.md
- trust-center-public-status.md

### Objection: “Do you have traction?”

Response:

> Ariyo should only claim traction where evidence exists. Early proof should be framed as pilot evidence, demo validation, customer discovery, usage signals, or implementation readiness unless production revenue exists.

Evidence to show:

- post-pilot investor proof pack
- pilot feedback report
- customer success metrics
- data room proof index

## 15. Follow-Up Rules

Follow-up must be specific.

Bad follow-up:

> Just checking in.

Better follow-up:

> Following up with the specific point we discussed: how Ariyo separates AI Employee creation from governance, workflow execution, and enterprise trust controls. I’m attaching the relevant 5-slide teaser and can walk through the demo if useful.

Follow-up cadence:

- first follow-up: 3–5 business days;
- second follow-up: 5–7 business days;
- final close-loop: 7–10 business days;
- nurture update: monthly or milestone-based.

## 18. RTL/LTR Law

Persian/Farsi and Arabic interfaces, outreach previews, CRM views, investor pipeline dashboards, data-room access pages, and localized investor/customer communications must be RTL and right-aligned across all UI elements.

English and European LTR interfaces, outreach previews, CRM views, investor pipeline dashboards, data-room access pages, and localized investor/customer communications must be LTR and left-aligned across all UI elements.

This rule applies to:

- investor CRM;
- outreach email previews;
- investor data room UI;
- fundraising dashboards;
- founder update templates;
- investor proof reports;
- localized pitch pages;
- data room permission screens.

If any other instruction conflicts with this layout law, the RTL/LTR law wins.

## 19. Forbidden Patterns

Ariyo must not:

- mass-email investors without segmentation;
- send decks without context;
- claim traction from internal demo data;
- share private pilot evidence publicly;
- promise compliance certification before evidence exists;

- invent customer quotes;
- inflate AI capability;
- hide production-readiness gaps;
