# Ariyo Board & Investor Governance Rhythm

## Core Contract

Ariyo Board & Investor Governance Rhythm is not a generic board meeting checklist, founder update habit, investor memo template, or governance bureaucracy.

It is the governed operating cadence that keeps Ariyo's founders, board members, advisors, investors, executive operators, finance owners, product leaders, customer leaders, and trust/governance stakeholders aligned after fundraising and during scale.

Ariyo must not let governance become passive reporting.

Governance must create clearer decisions, stronger accountability, better use of capital, faster risk visibility, and measurable progress toward enterprise-ready AI Employee Operating System execution.

## Why This Skill Exists

After fundraising, Ariyo needs more than product velocity. It needs a repeatable rhythm for:

- translating capital into accountable milestones;
- keeping investors informed without creating noise;
- separating operational updates from board-level decisions;
- reviewing product, revenue, runway, customer, marketplace, governance, and launch readiness together;
- escalating risks before they become surprises;
- documenting decision rights;
- maintaining investor trust after the round closes;
- using advisors intentionally rather than casually;
- ensuring Codex-built work connects to real business accountability.

This skill defines the cadence, artifacts, roles, agenda structure, KPIs, decision gates, and reporting rules for Ariyo's post-funding governance rhythm.

## Governance Layers

Ariyo governance is split into five layers. Codex, operators, and founders must not mix these layers.

### Layer 1: Weekly Operating Review

Purpose: run the company.

Participants:

- founders;
- product owner;
- engineering/frontend owner;
- customer success owner;
- revenue/fundraising owner;
- finance/operator owner;
- trust/compliance owner when relevant.

Cadence: weekly.

Primary question:

> Are we converting capital, product work, customer feedback, and pilot evidence into measurable progress this week?

### Layer 2: Biweekly Product & Customer Evidence Review

Purpose: connect product execution to customer proof.

Participants:

- founders;
- product/engineering;
- customer success;
- pilot owner;
- marketplace owner;
- operations owner.

Cadence: every two weeks.

Primary question:

> What evidence from users, pilots, demos, workflow usage, marketplace behavior, and support signals should change what we build next?

### Layer 3: Monthly Investor Update

Purpose: communicate progress, risks, asks, and proof to investors.

Participants:

- founders write;
- investor recipients read;
- key advisors may be copied depending on confidentiality level.

Cadence: monthly.

Primary question:

> What has materially changed, what evidence supports it, what risks are being managed, and what help do we need?

### Layer 4: Quarterly Board / Advisor Review

Purpose: make strategic decisions and review company trajectory.

Participants:

- founders;
- board members;
- lead investors where appropriate;
- strategic advisors where appropriate;
- finance/legal counsel when needed.

Cadence: quarterly.

Primary question:

> Are strategy, capital, product, customer evidence, team, runway, and risk posture aligned with Ariyo's next company milestone?

### Layer 5: Event-Triggered Governance Review

Purpose: handle major decisions or risks outside cadence.

Triggered by:

- major security incident;
- major production outage;
- enterprise pilot failure or major success;
- term-sheet or strategic acquisition approach;
- runway risk;
- pricing or packaging reset;
- founder/team change;
- major compliance risk;
- major customer expansion opportunity;
- product pivot consideration;
- marketplace trust/safety crisis.

Primary question:

> What decision is required now, who owns it, what evidence exists, and what must be communicated?

## Non-Negotiable Governance Rules

1. Ariyo must separate reporting from decision-making.
2. Every board-level discussion must have an owner and desired decision.
3. Every metric must include context, trend, and interpretation.
4. Every risk must include severity, owner, mitigation, and date of next review.
5. Every investor update must be evidence-backed, not claim-backed.
6. Every use-of-funds update must tie spending to milestones.
7. Every customer claim must trace to pilot evidence, usage, support record, or signed commitment.
8. Every production readiness claim must trace to release gates and gap audit status.
9. Every AI governance claim must trace to safety, permissions, audit, incident, or observability evidence.
10. Every RTL/LTR product surface shown to board or investors must respect Ariyo's global layout law.

## Global RTL/LTR Governance Law

Persian/Farsi and Arabic interfaces must be RTL and right-aligned across all UI elements.

English and European LTR interfaces must be LTR and left-aligned across all UI elements.

This applies to:

- board materials;
- investor updates;
- dashboards;
- screenshots;
- product demos;
- pitch materials;
- data room documents;
- pilot reports;
- EBR reports;
- financial summaries;
- customer proof screenshots;
- mockups and Figma artifacts;
- Codex-generated UI and reports.

If a board or investor material shows Persian or Arabic UI in LTR form, it is not presentation-ready.

## Weekly Operating Review

### Purpose

The weekly operating review keeps Ariyo execution honest. It should not become a long status meeting. It should answer what changed, what is blocked, what needs a decision, and what proof was created.

### Required Agenda

1. Scorecard snapshot
2. Last week's commitments
3. Product execution progress
4. Customer/pilot evidence
5. Revenue/fundraising pipeline
6. Production readiness risks
7. Marketplace health
8. Hiring/people needs
9. Finance/runway snapshot
10. Decisions required
11. Next week's commitments

### Required Scorecard

The weekly scorecard must include:

- product milestones completed;
- Codex work units completed;
- open product blockers;
- pilot/customer progress;
- active AI Employees in demo/pilot;
- active workflows configured;
- marketplace templates demo-ready;
- investor conversations advanced;
- current runway estimate;
- top three risks;
- next critical release gate.

### Weekly Review Output

At the end of each weekly review, Ariyo must produce:

```md

Date:

Owner:

## What changed this week
-

## Evidence created
-

## Decisions made
-

## Blockers
-

## Risks
-

## Next week's commitments
-

## Items for investor/board visibility
-
```

### Investor Update Rules

- Do not include vanity metrics without explanation.
- Do not announce production readiness unless release gates support it.
- Do not share customer names unless permission exists.
- Do not share confidential pilot data broadly.
- Do not overstate AI automation capabilities.
- Do not imply compliance certification without evidence.
- Do not confuse demo progress with revenue progress.
- Do not use raw screenshots if RTL/LTR behavior is wrong.

## Decision Rights

### Founder-Owned Decisions

- weekly execution priorities;
- product sequencing within approved scope;
- customer onboarding details;
- minor hiring sequencing;
- investor outreach cadence;
- demo narrative iteration;
- non-material pricing tests.

### Board/Investor Visibility Decisions

- material runway changes;
- change in round strategy;
- enterprise launch readiness;
- major security/compliance incident;
- major pricing or packaging change;
- founder compensation changes;
- strategic partnership with material obligations;
- hiring senior leadership;
- production launch decision.

### Board/Investor Decision or Consent Decisions

Depending on formal agreements, these may require board or investor approval:

- new financing round;
- major debt or financing instrument;
- sale/acquisition;
- significant equity grants;
- major budget deviation;
- material legal dispute;
- major strategic pivot;
- significant change to governance structure.

This skill does not replace legal documents. It defines internal operating awareness.

## Advisor Rhythm

Advisors must be used intentionally.

### Advisor Types

- product advisor;
- AI/technical advisor;
- enterprise sales advisor;
- marketplace advisor;
- security/compliance advisor;
- fundraising advisor;
- legal/finance advisor.

### Advisor Use Rules

- each advisor must have a defined domain;
- each advisor engagement must have a clear ask;
- advisory calls must produce decisions, introductions, reviews, or risk reduction;
- advisor feedback must not override customer evidence without review;
- advisors must not receive confidential data without permission.

### Advisor Meeting Output

```md
# Advisor Session Notes

Advisor:

Domain:

## Advice received
-

## Evidence reviewed
-

## Decisions influenced
-

## Follow-up actions
-

## Owner
-
```

## Investor Ask System

Every investor ask must be specific.

Bad asks:

- "Any feedback?"
- "Any intros?"
- "What do you think?"

Good asks:

- "Can you introduce us to 2 enterprise operators managing AI adoption in finance or HR?"
- "Can you review whether this proof pack is strong enough for seed-stage diligence?"
- "Can you introduce one investor who has backed vertical AI workflow platforms?"
- "Can you pressure-test our pricing narrative for AI Employee capacity?"

## Data Room Freshness Rhythm

The investor data room must be reviewed monthly.

Required monthly checks:

- pitch deck current;
- demo video/screenshots current;
- pilot proof current;
- financial model current;
- runway snapshot current;
- product roadmap current;
- release readiness current;
- trust/compliance evidence current;
- legal docs current;
- customer evidence permissions current.

If data room artifacts are stale, investor updates must not reference them as current.

## Report Quality Bar

Every governance report must be:

- concise;
- evidence-backed;
- owner-assigned;
- date-stamped;
- decision-oriented;
- risk-aware;
- not overly optimistic;
- not vague;
- not vanity-driven;
- not detached from product, customer, or financial reality.

## Forbidden Patterns

Ariyo must not:

- send investor updates with vague progress statements;
- present screenshots that violate RTL/LTR rules;
- hide material risks until board meetings;
- use advisors without clear asks;
- treat investor interest as capital committed;
- treat pilot conversations as traction;
- treat demo completion as production readiness;
- treat data room uploads as diligence readiness;
- discuss valuation without evidence and readiness;
- build governance dashboards that only decorate metrics;
- let Codex invent fake investor, customer, financial, or legal facts.
