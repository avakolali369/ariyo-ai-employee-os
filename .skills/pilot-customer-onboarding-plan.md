# Ariyo Pilot Customer Onboarding Plan

## Core Contract

Ariyo Pilot Customer Onboarding Plan is not a generic customer onboarding checklist, customer-success playbook, sales handoff document, or implementation calendar.

It is the governed plan that defines how Ariyo selects, prepares, activates, supports, measures, learns from, and graduates the first pilot customers for the Ariyo AI Employee Operating System.

A pilot customer must not be treated as an experiment without structure.

A pilot customer is a controlled proof environment where Ariyo validates real customer value, AI Employee usefulness, workflow reliability, governance expectations, support load, marketplace readiness, trust objections, and commercial expansion potential.

If a pilot does not produce evidence, the pilot did not happen.

---

## 1. Purpose

This skill tells founders, product owners, sales teams, customer success teams, implementation partners, support operators, frontend engineers, backend engineers, Codex, and AI-assisted builders how Ariyo must prepare and run the first pilot customer onboarding motion.

The plan exists to prevent four common failures:

1. onboarding the wrong customers too early,
2. promising production readiness before Ariyo is ready,
3. turning pilots into open-ended custom projects,
4. failing to capture evidence that supports product, investor, enterprise, and go-to-market decisions.

Ariyo pilots must validate the operating model of AI Employees, not just show a dashboard.

## 2. Non-Negotiable Pilot Principles

### 2.1 Pilot is not production

Pilot customers may experience controlled limitations, guided setup, manual support, restricted features, and staged rollout.

Ariyo must clearly communicate:

- what is live,
- what is simulated,
- what is assisted by the Ariyo team,
- what is not yet production-ready,
- what feedback is expected,
- what success means.

### 2.2 Pilot is outcome-first

Every pilot must have 1-3 measurable business outcomes.

Examples:

- reduce manual support triage time,
- speed up lead qualification,
- improve internal knowledge retrieval,
- automate approval routing,
- reduce operational backlog,
- improve customer onboarding response quality.

A pilot without measurable outcome targets is not allowed.

### 2.3 Pilot scope must be locked

The pilot scope must be defined before onboarding begins.

No pilot may expand into unlimited custom work without a scope-change decision.

### 2.4 Every pilot has an owner

Ariyo must assign:

- customer owner,
- Ariyo pilot owner,
- implementation owner,
- support owner,
- technical escalation owner,
- executive sponsor if needed.

### 2.5 Every pilot creates evidence

Evidence may include:

- before/after workflow comparison,
- time saved,
- adoption metrics,
- usage logs,
- AI Employee performance reports,
- customer quotes,
- objection logs,
- incident logs,
- security review notes,
- expansion opportunities,
- churn risks.

## 3. Pilot Customer Selection Criteria

Ariyo must not onboard every interested customer as a pilot.

### 3.1 Ideal pilot customer profile

A strong pilot customer should have:

- a real operational workflow with measurable pain,
- a motivated internal champion,
- a decision-maker or sponsor who understands the pilot goal,
- access to usable knowledge/data sources,
- willingness to provide feedback weekly,
- tolerance for controlled MVP limitations,
- no immediate demand for unsupported enterprise guarantees,
- one clear department or workflow to start with.

### 3.2 Good pilot verticals

Recommended early pilot categories:

- customer support operations,
- sales operations,
- onboarding operations,
- internal knowledge management,
- HR/admin operations,
- finance/accounting support workflows,
- marketplace/template-assisted business operations.

### 3.3 Avoid early pilot customers that require

Do not choose early pilots that require:

- complex custom integrations before core validation,
- high-risk legal/medical/financial decision automation,
- regulated production deployment without legal review,
- extremely large enterprise procurement before trust assets are ready,
- custom AI model training as a prerequisite,
- unsupported language coverage,
- unrestricted agent autonomy,
- hard SLA guarantees before reliability is validated.

## 4. Pilot Readiness Requirements Before Onboarding

Before a customer enters pilot onboarding, Ariyo must confirm readiness across these areas.

### 4.1 Product readiness

Required:

- MVP surface available or demo-ready,
- dashboard available,
- AI Employee Builder available or guided setup available,
- AI Employee Detail available,
- Workflow Builder available or guided workflow setup available,
- Marketplace template selection available or curated manually,
- Trust Center preview available,
- Pricing/plan explanation available,
- Launch Readiness view available internally.

### 4.2 Technical readiness

Required:

- demo data replaced with pilot-specific data where appropriate,
- mock API boundaries understood,
- backend/API gaps documented,
- data import path defined,
- user account creation path defined,
- audit logs available at least in basic form,
- incident escalation path defined,
- rollback/pause path defined.

### 4.3 Customer success readiness

Required:

- onboarding checklist prepared,
- weekly check-in agenda prepared,
- feedback form prepared,
- pilot success criteria agreed,
- support channel created,
- escalation rules documented,
- pilot closeout review template prepared.

### 4.4 Trust readiness

Required:

- plain-language data handling explanation,
- permission scope explanation,
- AI limitations explanation,
- human review rules,
- security contact path,
- incident communication path,
- no unsupported compliance claims.

## 5. Pilot Onboarding Flow

### Phase 0 — Pre-Qualification

Goal: decide whether the customer should enter pilot.

Inputs:

- customer profile,
- use case description,
- workflow pain,
- decision-maker involvement,
- data sensitivity,
- required integrations,
- requested timeline,
- success expectation.

Output:

- accepted,
- deferred,
- rejected,
- requires enterprise review.

Codex-facing note:

If building onboarding UI, this phase maps to qualification forms, account notes, risk labels, and pilot status fields.

### Phase 1 — Discovery Call

Goal: understand the workflow and business outcome.

Questions:

- What process is currently painful?
- Who performs the work today?
- What tools are used?
- What data/knowledge is needed?
- What does success look like after 30 days?
- What decisions must remain human-approved?
- What risks or compliance constraints matter?
- Who will use the AI Employee?
- Who can approve launch?

Output:

- pilot outcome statement,
- workflow candidate,
- AI Employee candidate,
- success metrics,
- risk notes,
- pilot owner map.

### Phase 2 — Pilot Scope Lock

Goal: freeze the first pilot scope.

Must define:

- selected use case,
- selected AI Employee role,
- selected workflow,
- required knowledge sources,
- approved tools/integrations,
- user roles,
- pilot duration,
- success metrics,
- support model,
- excluded requests.

Ariyo must document what will not be built during the pilot.

### Phase 3 — Workspace Setup

Goal: prepare the customer workspace.

Setup items:

- organization name,
- workspace name,
- users,
- roles,
- plan type,
- language/locale,
- timezone,
- permission groups,
- pilot mode label,
- data residency expectation,
- support contacts.

### Phase 4 — AI Employee Setup

Goal: create the first AI Employee.

Required configuration:

- AI Employee name,
- job role,
- owner,
- outcomes,
- allowed actions,
- restricted actions,
- knowledge sources,
- memory scope,
- tools,
- approval requirements,
- escalation path,
- cost guardrails,
- test scenarios.

### Phase 5 — Workflow Setup

Goal: attach the AI Employee to one real workflow.

Workflow must include:

- trigger,
- steps,
- AI Employee assignment,
- human review gate,
- fallback path,
- exception path,
- completion outcome,
- audit log event,
- reporting metric.

### Phase 6 — Simulation and Review

Goal: test before exposing to live users.

Simulation must test:

- happy path,
- incomplete data,
- wrong input,
- risky request,
- permission boundary,
- escalation path,
- failure handling,
- cost warning,
- audit logging.

Output:

- simulation report,
- risks found,
- changes needed,
- launch approval decision.

### Phase 7 — Pilot Launch

Goal: launch the pilot in controlled mode.

Launch must include:

- launch owner,
- customer contact,
- support channel,
- launch date,
- first-week monitoring plan,
- rollback/pause process,
- first checkpoint date,
- success metrics baseline.

### Phase 8 — Weekly Pilot Review

Goal: measure adoption and improve.

Review agenda:

- usage summary,
- outcome progress,
- unresolved issues,
- AI Employee quality review,
- workflow friction,
- customer feedback,
- support tickets,
- incidents,
- requested changes,
- scope discipline,
- next actions.

### Phase 9 — Pilot Closeout

Goal: decide what happens next.

Possible outcomes:

- convert to paid plan,
- extend pilot,
- expand to another workflow,
- pause for product gaps,
- reject use case,
- create case study candidate,
- escalate to enterprise pilot.

## 7. Pilot Roles and Ownership

### Customer roles

- Executive Sponsor: validates business value.
- Pilot Champion: drives usage internally.
- Admin: configures users and policies.
- Operator: uses AI Employee/workflow daily.
- Reviewer: approves AI outputs or escalations.
- Security/IT Reviewer: reviews data and access.

### Ariyo roles

- Pilot Owner: accountable for the pilot outcome.
- Implementation Lead: configures the workflow and AI Employee.
- Product Owner: captures product gaps.
- Support Owner: handles issues.
- Engineering Owner: handles technical blockers.
- Trust Owner: handles security/privacy/compliance concerns.

## 9. Pilot Support Path

Support must be structured.

### Support levels

- Level 1: usage questions and onboarding help.
- Level 2: workflow or AI Employee configuration issue.
- Level 3: data, permission, integration, or policy issue.
- Level 4: incident, security concern, or production-blocking issue.

### Required support fields

Every support issue must track:

- customer,
- workspace,
- user,
- AI Employee,
- workflow,
- issue type,
- severity,
- owner,
- status,
- related evidence,
- resolution,
- follow-up action.

## 10. Feedback Loop

Pilot feedback must be converted into structured product decisions.

Feedback categories:

- product usability,
- AI quality,
- workflow design,
- marketplace fit,
- governance concern,
- trust concern,
- performance/reliability,
- pricing/commercial,
- support/onboarding.

Each feedback item must be classified as:

- fix now,
- improve next sprint,
- backlog later,
- not in Ariyo scope,
- customer education issue,
- strategic product insight.

## 11. Pilot Boundaries

### Allowed during pilot

- guided setup,
- limited feature scope,
- manual support assistance,
- curated Marketplace templates,
- controlled workflow launch,
- manual evidence collection,
- limited integrations,
- controlled AI Employee autonomy.

### Not allowed during pilot

- unlimited custom development,
- unsupported compliance promises,
- unrestricted AI actions,
- customer-specific product forks,
- hidden human labor presented as automation,
- fake metrics,
- production claims without release-gate approval,
- AI decisions without review where risk is high.

---

## 12. Demo-to-Pilot Boundary

Ariyo must clearly distinguish demo and pilot.

| Area | Demo | Pilot |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Data | seeded/demo | customer-specific or controlled real data |
| Users | fictional/persona | real customer users |
| Metrics | illustrative | measured |
| Support | presenter-led | structured support path |
| AI Employee | simulated or configured | customer-scoped and monitored |
| Workflow | guided | real operational workflow |
| Evidence | narrative | captured proof |
| Risk | low | reviewed and owned |

## 17. Localization and RTL/LTR Law

Ariyo pilot onboarding surfaces must obey the global layout law:

- Persian/Farsi and Arabic interfaces must be RTL and right-aligned across all UI elements.
- English and European LTR interfaces must be LTR and left-aligned across all UI elements.
- Direction must affect layout, navigation, forms, tables, sidebars, charts, cards, empty states, support messages, onboarding checklists, reports, and email/message previews.
- Do not create mirrored inconsistencies where text is RTL but layout remains LTR.
- Do not translate proper names, model names, plan names, internal IDs, or technical identifiers unless product localization explicitly requires it.

## 19. Codex Stop Rules

Codex must stop and report if:

- pilot scope requires unsupported backend APIs,
- customer data requires unapproved sensitive handling,
- feature requires production-level compliance claims,
- AI Employee autonomy exceeds approved policy,
- user role or permission model is unclear,
- RTL/LTR behavior is missing,
- demo data and pilot data conflict,
- implementation requires hidden manual work presented as automation.

## 21. Golden Rule

Do not onboard a pilot customer to prove Ariyo looks impressive.
